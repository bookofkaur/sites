FAFSA From Start to Finish: A First-Gen Student's Complete Guide

By Darrian Belcher6 min readFinancial Aid · FAFSA

Nobody in my family had filled out a FAFSA before. I had to figure it out myself — through research, trial and error, and a lot of asking for help. I'm writing this so you don't have to start from zero the way I did.

This is the guide I wish I'd had.

What Is FAFSA and Why Does It Matter?

FAFSA stands for Free Application for Federal Student Aid. It's the form the U.S. government uses to determine how much financial aid you qualify for — including grants (free money), work-study (earn while you study), and subsidized loans (borrow at lower rates).

Here's the thing: you cannot get federal financial aid without it. And many states and colleges use your FAFSA to determine their own aid too. Not filing it is leaving money on the table.

Key Dates to Know

  • FAFSA opens: October 1st every year for the following academic year
  • Federal deadline: June 30th — but don't wait that long
  • State deadlines: Often February or March — check your state's specific date
  • School deadlines: Many schools run out of institutional aid early — file as soon as FAFSA opens
💡 File as early as possible. Financial aid is often first-come, first-served. October 1st is when FAFSA opens — that's when you file, not when you start thinking about it.

What You'll Need

Gather these before you sit down to fill it out:

  • Your Social Security number (and a parent's, if you're a dependent)
  • Your FSA ID — create one at studentaid.gov (both you and a parent need separate ones)
  • Tax returns from two years ago (the "prior-prior year" — so for fall 2026 entry, that's 2024 taxes)
  • W-2 forms for you and your parents
  • Bank and savings account balances
  • Records of any untaxed income (child support, veterans benefits, etc.)

The IRS Data Retrieval Tool Is Your Best Friend

When you get to the tax section on FAFSA, use the IRS Data Retrieval Tool (DRT) to pull your tax info automatically. It reduces errors, speeds up processing, and reduces the chance you'll be asked to verify your information later. Use it.

List Your Schools Strategically

You can list up to 20 colleges on your FAFSA. List your state's schools first — many states prioritize students who list in-state schools early. Don't worry about the order revealing your preferences to colleges; they can't see each other's spots on your list.

After You Submit: What to Expect

You'll receive a Student Aid Report (SAR) within a few days to a few weeks. Check it carefully for errors. It will show your Student Aid Index (SAI) — the number schools use to determine your financial need. A lower number means more potential aid.

Once you're accepted to colleges, they'll send financial aid award letters. These will break down grants, scholarships, work-study, and loans. More grants = better. Loans must be repaid. Compare these offers carefully before committing.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Waiting until spring to file (state aid may already be gone)
  • Not creating your FSA ID in advance (it can take days to verify)
  • Confusing the CSS Profile with FAFSA (some private schools require both)
  • Not updating your FAFSA if your family's financial situation changes significantly
  • Thinking you make "too much" to qualify — file anyway, you may be surprised
