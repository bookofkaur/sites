Why Internships Are the Most Important Thing You Can Do in College

By Darrian Belcher5 min readCareer · Internships

A degree in IT or computer science opens the door to an enormous range of careers. Your responsibilities could range from managing patient records at a hospital to developing apps that change how millions of people live. With that many paths, figuring out which one is yours is one of the hardest parts of college.

That's exactly why internships are irreplaceable.

The Classroom Teaches You Skills. Internships Teach You Who You Are.

You can learn to write code in a lecture hall. But you can't learn whether you want to work in a startup vs. a corporation, whether you thrive in collaborative environments or prefer deep solo focus, or whether your passion is in the technical or the business side — until you actually do the work.

Internships compress years of trial-and-error into a summer. That's an incredible deal.

What You Actually Gain

  • Clarity on your career path. Talking to people in different roles at the same company is one of the fastest ways to narrow down what you want to spend your life doing.
  • A network that matters. The people you meet at internships become references, mentors, and sometimes co-founders. Your network is often your net worth.
  • Real skills. Classroom projects are simulated. Work projects have real deadlines, real stakes, and real feedback — which makes you learn faster.
  • A competitive resume. Recruiters consistently rank internship experience as one of the most important factors when hiring new graduates.

Dream Big: Intern Abroad

I've always been drawn to the idea of interning internationally — fixing computers by day and exploring a new country by night. Interning abroad introduces you to problems and cultures that don't exist in your hometown, and the solutions you develop become more globally-minded because of it.

"Interning abroad would introduce me to a global network of contacts in the technology field that I can create lasting relationships with and learn from."

Whether it's a large corporation overseas or a small local tech startup in another country, the experience will develop you personally and professionally in ways that no classroom can replicate.

How to Actually Land Internships

Start earlier than you think you should.

Most competitive internship programs have deadlines in the fall for the following summer. Freshman year is not too early to start researching.

Use targeted programs.

Programs like the DOE Minority Internship, Google STEP, Microsoft Explore, and countless others recruit specifically from underrepresented groups. Don't overlook these — they're often less competitive and more supportive.

Build your portfolio as you go.

GitHub, personal projects, hackathons — these signal to recruiters that you're serious about your craft even outside of class.

Reach out directly.

A well-crafted cold email to someone doing work you admire — with a specific question, not just "can I intern?" — works more often than people think.

💡 Reality Check: You won't get every internship you apply to. Apply to 20 and treat each rejection as data. What can you improve? What skills are interviewers asking about that you don't have yet? Use the gaps to guide what you learn next.
