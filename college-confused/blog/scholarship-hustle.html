How to Find and Win Scholarships: My Honest Strategy

By Darrian Belcher5 min readFinancial Aid · Scholarships

I applied to the Gates Scholarship. The Cooke Scholarship. HBCU honors programs. DOE internship awards. State scholarships. Local scholarships. Some I won. Some I didn't. All of them taught me something.

Here's the honest strategy — including what actually moves the needle and what doesn't.

The Mindset Shift First

Stop thinking of scholarship applications as a lottery. They're not. The students who win scholarships consistently aren't the luckiest — they're the ones who understand what each scholarship is actually looking for and write directly to that.

Every scholarship has a mission. The Gates Scholarship wants students who will make a difference in their communities. STEM-focused awards want students committed to those fields. Local community scholarships want students who are rooted in and giving back to their area. Find the mission, then show how you live it.

Where to Find Scholarships

Start local.

Local scholarships from community foundations, churches, businesses, and civic organizations have far fewer applicants than national ones. Your odds are dramatically better. Ask your school counselor, check with local banks and credit unions, and search "[your city] scholarship" and "[your county] scholarship."

Go to your school's financial aid office.

Many colleges have institutional scholarships that don't get widely advertised. Some are based on merit, some on need, some on specific demographics or majors. Ask directly: "What scholarships do you have for students like me?"

Search by identity and major.

  • First-generation college student scholarships
  • African American / Black student scholarships (UNCF, HBCU-specific awards)
  • STEM scholarships (NSF, DOE, NSBE, SHPE)
  • State-based merit programs

Use reputable databases.

Fastweb, Scholarships.com, Bold.org, and the College Board's scholarship search are all legitimate and free. Avoid anything that asks you to pay to apply — real scholarships never charge fees.

How to Write a Winning Essay

Most scholarships require an essay. Here's what separates winners from everyone else:

  • Answer the actual question. Sounds obvious. Many students write a great essay that doesn't address the prompt. Read it three times before you start writing.
  • Be specific about your cause. "I want to help people" is not a cause. "I want to build technology tools that help first-gen students navigate the college application process, because I was one and I know the gap" — that's a cause.
  • Connect your past to your future. Show how your experiences made you who you are and point toward what you're going to do. Scholarships are investments — help them see the ROI.
  • Get feedback before submitting. A teacher, counselor, or mentor who will tell you the truth is worth more than ten friends who just say it's great.

The Volume Game

Apply to more than you think you need. A reasonable goal is 15–25 scholarship applications over the course of your senior year. The essays you write for one often overlap with others — build a library of strong responses to common prompts and adapt them.

"With no renters insurance, we had to replace everything ourselves. This scholarship won't just fund my education — it will be an investment in my family."

That sentence, from one of my personal scholarship essays, made the stakes real. Don't be afraid to make the stakes real.

💡 Track Everything. Keep a spreadsheet (or use the Scholarship Tracker in the College Confused tools) with the name, amount, deadline, requirements, and status of every scholarship you apply to. Missing a deadline because you lost track is the most avoidable mistake there is.
