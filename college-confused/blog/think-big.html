Think Big, Work Hard, Achieve: The Philosophy That Got Me Through College Apps

By Darrian Belcher3 min readMindset · Goal-Setting

Three words. One framework. "Think Big, Work Hard, Achieve" sounds simple — maybe even cliché. But when I actually broke down what it means in practice, it became one of the most useful thinking tools I have.

Think Big

Whenever I set a goal, I start by thinking big — not just about the end result, but about everything required to get there. Short term and long term. Every obstacle I can anticipate. Every resource I'll need. I write it all down in a journal.

Thinking big isn't about being delusional. It's about refusing to limit your vision before you've even started. Most people narrow their goals before they've tested the ceiling. Think big first. Reality will tell you what to adjust.

Work Hard

The middle part of any goal is unglamorous. Nobody posts about the 11pm study sessions, the rejection emails, the essays you scrapped and rewrote from scratch. But that's where everything actually happens.

Working hard means being consistent when you don't feel like it. It means building habits — not relying on motivation, which comes and goes. I learned this from track: you don't feel like training every day. You go anyway. The results show up later.

Achieve

When you reach a goal, don't just move on immediately. Reflect. What did you do that worked? What would you do differently? The ability to extract lessons from your own experience and apply them to the next challenge is one of the most valuable skills you can develop.

"When I finally achieve my goal, I reflect on the steps I took so I can apply them again when needed."

How to Apply This to College Applications

  • Think Big: Map out every school you want to apply to, every essay prompt, every deadline. Don't let logistics surprise you in October.
  • Work Hard: Start your essays early. Revise ruthlessly. Ask for feedback. Treat each application like it's your only one.
  • Achieve: When acceptances start coming in, take stock of what you did well. When rejections come — and some will — ask what you can learn and apply to the next thing.
💡 Journaling Tip: Writing your goals down makes them 42% more likely to happen, according to research by Dr. Gail Matthews at Dominican University. Don't keep your goals in your head. Put them on paper. Then work backwards from the finish line.
