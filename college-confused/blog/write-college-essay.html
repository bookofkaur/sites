How to Write a College Essay That Actually Gets You In

By Darrian Belcher7 min readApplications · Essays

I've written college essays for Carnegie Mellon, NC A&T, the Gates Scholarship, the Cooke Scholarship, the Department of Energy internship, and dozens of other programs. Some of them worked. Some of them didn't. Here's everything I learned.

What Admissions Officers Are Actually Looking For

They're not looking for the most impressive accomplishment. They're not looking for the student who had the hardest life, or the student who's going to cure cancer, or the student with the most perfect grammar.

They're looking for a person. A specific, real, three-dimensional human being who they can picture walking across campus and contributing something to their community.

Your essay is the only part of the application that is entirely yours. Use it to be unmistakably yourself.

The Most Common Mistake

Generic. Students write generic essays. "I've always loved technology." "Science fascinates me." "I want to make the world a better place." These sentences could have been written by 10,000 other applicants.

The antidote is specificity. Don't say you love technology — describe the moment in freshman year when you finally got a loop to work after two hours of debugging and felt a rush you'd never felt before. That's a person. That's a story. That's what they remember.

Structure That Works

Open with a scene, not a summary.

Drop the reader into a moment. "My heart pounded with excitement as I approached the starting line" is more engaging than "Running track taught me a lot about discipline." Start in the middle of something happening.

Build from the specific to the universal.

Start with one concrete moment or detail, then zoom out to what it means. What does breaking your 200m record say about how you approach challenges? What does a house fire say about your relationship to family and resilience?

Show, don't tell.

Don't tell me you're resilient. Show me the moment you chose to keep going when you had every reason to stop. The reader should feel the character trait — not just be told about it.

End with what you're carrying forward.

The best essays end with a forward-facing idea. Not "and that's why I'm great" — but "and here's what I'm going to do with everything I've learned." Connect your past to your future.

Prompts to Know Cold

  • "Why this school?" — Research specific programs, professors, and initiatives. Show them you've done homework. Vague flattery doesn't work.
  • "Describe a challenge you've overcome." — Show what you did, not just what happened to you. Action matters.
  • "Who has had the most impact on you?" — Be honest, not impressive. The teacher who actually changed you beats the famous mentor you met once.
  • "What are your career plans?" — Tell a story of growth with a mission, not just a list of job titles.
  • "Tell us about an extracurricular." — Don't describe the activity. Describe who you became because of it.

Edit Like You Mean It

Your first draft is almost never your best essay. Write it, let it breathe for 48 hours, then come back and cut anything that doesn't earn its place. Then get someone else to read it — a teacher, counselor, or mentor — and ask them: "Do you see ME in this essay? Does anything feel generic?"

The goal is an essay that could only have been written by you. If someone else could have written it, rewrite it.

💡 Word Count: The Common App main essay is 650 words. Use all of it — but don't pad. Every sentence should earn its place. If you're at 400 words and you've said everything, you haven't said enough. Go deeper.

One More Thing

Don't write what you think they want to hear. Write what's true. The essays that got me the most opportunities weren't the most polished — they were the most honest. Vulnerability, when grounded in growth, is the most compelling thing you can put on a page.
